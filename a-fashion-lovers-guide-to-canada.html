A fashion-lover’s guide to Canada

Wednesday 22 November 2017 15.22 GMT

What I knew about Canada before I embarked on my one-week, three-city trip this summer was limited to just a handful of sources: the 90s TV show North of 60, set in the fictitious Northwest Territories town of Lynx River; my favourite Drake songs, which can arguably be thought of as a Toronto guidebook written by its most famous resident; and the work of iconic brands such as Roots and Herschel.

What I didn’t know was that by the time I left, I’d have discovered one of the best crops of talented local makers and designers anywhere in the world, eaten the best tacos of my life, and experienced a view – on the summit of Vancouver’s Howe Sound – that made me feel cosmically tiny and insignificant in the best possible way. And that’s to say nothing of the incredible people I met, and the raw energy of its diverse, accepting and multicultural cities. Canada really is a place without barriers – somewhere no one could feel like an outsider.

Toronto

I arrived in Toronto with high expectations, and I can honestly say that they were surpassed. World-class boutiques, killer vintage finds, and an unmistakable big town buzz. This is a city that definitely has its pulse on contemporary fashion.

Ice-cream and being seen on Queen Street West
Queen Street West is undoubtedly Toronto’s creative and fashion hub, and somewhere that has been on my radar since Vogue crowned it one of the world’s top three hippest neighbourhoods in 2014. And it’s easy to see why. Not only is it home to high-end destination boutiques, such as Nomad, but it’s also a place that’s giving a platform for Toronto’s homegrown talent – there are a lot of independent boutiques supporting each other in this prime location.

I discovered local makers such as YNOT and Kotn, who are both representing everything that’s great about the contemporary manifestation of Toronto as a city and Canada as a country with their designs. YNOT’s sturdy, stylish rucksacks and hardwearing jackets really call to mind just how connected to nature you are in Canada – even in the middle of its biggest city – while Kotn’s super clean and elegant basics represent luxury in its simplest form.

Those looking to pair their local selections with some high-end international pieces would be advised to head to Nomad, which stocks an excellent range of powerhouse names such as Comme des Garçons and Raf Simons. My top Queen Street West tip? Take a long stroll, heading west to east, with an ice-cream in hand from iHalo Krunch.

Vintage finds in Kensington Market
There’s nowhere better to go for vintage than Kensington Market. This hip, colourful neighbourhood just off downtown Toronto has a plethora of stores full of wonderful finds. It has an amazing sense of street life: turn any random corner and you’ll find bars with small outdoor performance spaces, full of drinkers enjoying a show in the sun, or – as we did – dining at truly quirky restaurants, such as Rasta Pasta, the home of Jamaican-Italian fusion.

Seeing where the magic happens at the Roots factory
Roots is probably the quintessential Canadian brand. Again, the strong connection to nature really comes through in what it produces. As a brand, Roots has been a part of pop culture for such a long time, but even with that long-running legacy it still manages to be super current – proved by its recent collaboration with Drake, who had a range of varsity jackets for his OVO label co-produced with the brand.

I had the chance to visit the factory, which was fascinating from my perspective as a tailor and designer, to get an insight into its production processes. One thing is for sure: they really know their leather. But it was also great to understand who makes it and why they make it. When you walk into the factory you see flags everywhere, representing all of the different nations Roots’ employees come from. It really reflects Canada’s multicultural vibe – people bring their own roots to Roots.

Cool Canadiana at Drake General Store
Just across the street from the Drake Hotel on Queen West, Drake General Store is like a souvenir shop for people who wouldn’t normally buy souvenirs. It’s a stylish space full of considered Canadiana, where all of the products have a really curated feel – from the beautiful Alberta-made blanket I picked up, to the artisan maple syrup I brought back for my friend who runs a cafe back home in south London. If you’re only going to go to Toronto but want to get a sense of the whole of Canada while you’re there, it’s definitely the place to go.

New architecture and timeless art at the Art Gallery of Ontario
I was really excited to see the recent Frank Gehry extension of the Art Gallery of Ontario; it’s a stunning addition to an already stunning building. Inside, I got to see some of the fantastic work by the Group of Seven – a group of Canadian landscape painters who initiated Canada’s first major national art movement.

Fring’s by name, fring by nature
Named and frequented by Drake, Fring’s delivers a unique fusion of Asian and soul food under the stewardship of Canadian celebrity chef Susur Lee. Fring’s derives its name from Canadian-Caribbean patois, translating roughly to “good times remembered” – and after one meal here I’ve got no reservations in saying that the moniker fits. It’s an intimate space with a first-rate interior fit out, and the Hong Kong chicken and waffles with maple chili glaze were unforgettable.

Montreal

If only I’d had more than two days in Montreal. It’s a city unlike any other I’ve been to, a perfect blend of European charm and North American hustle. It’s also a place for those who love the finer things in life: amazing food, slick bars and very stylish people.

Mont Royal
The size and scale of the green spaces in Canada’s cities is something that I still struggle to get my head around, and Montreal’s Mont Royal is no exception. The vista afforded over the city from the summit at the highest point of the park takes in all of downtown Montreal, while the walking route back downhill towards Saint-Laurent offers a great view of the nine-storey Leonard Cohen mural keeping watch over his old borough.

Sheer ‘jeanius’ at Naked & Famous Denim
Brandon Svarc is a denim genius. Producing jeans under his Naked & Famous moniker, he’s a maker who knows his product so well that he can stretch it completely to its limits – whether its dyeing jeans in green tea or red wine, or producing the heaviest pair of jeans in the world out of 32oz Japanese selvedge denim. The Naked & Famous factory is right on top of the store – the denim gets made on the third floor and then it’s sold downstairs in the Tate & Yoko boutique.

My idea of heaven at Maison Cloakroom
I wasn’t expecting this: a premium men’s tailoring retailer with its own speakeasy. In other words, something like my idea of heaven. Maison Cloakroom is housed in a beautiful 19th-century townhouse on Rue de la Montagne in downtown Montreal. This place sums up that distinctive Montreal feel – old and new, European and American – more than anywhere else in the city.

Meeting the makers at Lowell MTL
Lowell MTL may take its name from the region of Massachusetts that was once home to a large population of French Canadians – Jack Kerouac the most famous of them – but it is a Montreal brand to the core, with all of its products taking inspiration from locations in and around the city. Mathieu Mudie and Rachel Fortin, the husband and wife team behind Lowell MTL, made the brand’s name producing premium leather accessories, but they have since expanded into truly stylish men’s and women’s clothing ranges.

The ultimate Reuben at Dunn’s Famous
It probably goes without saying, but a classic smoked meat sandwich from one of Montreal’s famous delis is something you can’t leave the city without trying. As with any local specialty, there’s always hot debate about who does the best, but it’s hard to imagine anywhere outdoing the full-fat, classic Reuben at Dunn’s Famous – a sandwich piled impossibly high with Dunn’s famous smoked brisket, swiss cheese, sauerkraut and thousand island sauce.

Returning the favour at Lov
Sometimes you just stumble upon one of the best places in town. That’s what happened with Lov. At the end of the first day, we’d been walking around downtown Montreal looking for somewhere to eat and happened to find this place after taking a wrong turn en route to another restaurant. But it only took one look at what was coming out of the kitchen to convince us to stay – smart, innovative vegetarian dishes just asking to be washed down with one of Lov’s signature botanic cocktails. So good we returned the following night.

Vancouver

By now I should have been used to just how close to nature you are in any Canadian city, but Vancouver took it up a notch: after taking a cab downtown, I didn’t even realise that only 15 minutes walk from where we were staying you could be right on the water. It’s something you can feel with the relaxed, west coast attitude of the people, and something that really comes through in the work of local makers Herschel and Dayton Boots, who have put Vancouver on the world style map.

Colour and craftsmanship at the Museum of Anthropology
Set deep in the leafy campus of the University of British Columbia, the Museum of Anthropology is home to a vast collection of First Nations artworks and cultural artefacts. I was lucky enough to have met Haida designer and artist Dorothy Grant at her home and studio before my visit – she gave me a real understanding of the varying colours and shapes used by different tribes in First Nations art. Seeing the level of craftsmanship on the totems, masks, garments and weaves on display up close was an experience I won’t forget.

Revolutionary fabrics at Herschel
It’s great to see such a young brand doing what it does so confidently, and with such a great understanding of their product. Herschel bags have made their mark so strongly as a street style staple in recent years that, for me, they’ve become virtually synonymous with the idea of a rucksack. As a tailor, it was fascinating to see the work that the brand is doing in producing its own materials, such as the ApexKnit: a groundbreaking new fabric from which its makers are able to produce a single-piece rucksack.

A fine legacy at Dayton Boots
Dayton Boots represents so much of what is great about Vancouver’s makers and style ethos. The brand started out more than 70 years ago, making boots for loggers, and their product today is still as sturdy and well-crafted as it was then. But now, like so many great workwear brands, what they produce works just as well as a contemporary look. I’d recommend taking a look for yourself – the Dayton Boots factory is open to the public and right behind their flagship store on East Hastings Street.

Record shopping and more in Gastown
One thing I hadn’t had the chance to do in Canada before arriving in Vancouver was to go record shopping. Luckily for me it turns out that Gastown is home to one of the best record shops in the city – Beat Street Records, just around the corner from Gastown’s famous Steam clock.

I’m a lover of jazz, funk and hip-hop and was blown away by the selections: among them releases by J-Dilla and Slum Village that I’d never come across in London, and hard-to-come-by rarities by Thelonious Monk and a load of other jazz greats.

Of course, record shops aren’t the only thing you’ll find in Gastown. It’s a neighbourhood that has long been considered the spot for fine eating and drinking in Vancouver. Try Bao Down for some of Vancouver’s best Asian fusion street food.

No more tacos after Tacofino
And then there was Tacofino. What can I say about this place, other than is sells simply the finest tacos I’ve ever eaten. They were so good that I haven’t been able to find anything close since returning to London – it just feels like we’re not doing it right. But it’s not just the tacos – Tacofino also distinguishes itself with west coast-inspired ceviche, tostadas and nachos, as well as the phenomenal chilli salt wings with lime and valentina.

The view to end all views, from the Sea to Sky Gondola
If you’re going to Vancouver, you have to take the Sea to Sky Gondola. It takes you over some of the most incredible natural scenery I saw in Canada, and it’s all less than an hour’s drive away from the city centre. The view from the summit was simply awe-inspiring, and many of the hiking trails coursing up the ascent were easily walkable for a beginner like me. It’s one of those places that makes you and your problems feel small – and all the better for it.
